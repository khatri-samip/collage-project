Mount Everest

The tallest peak in the world at 8,848 meters, drawing adventurers and climbers from across the globe.

Physical and Geographical Details

Official Height: 8,848.86 meters (29,031.7 feet) above sea level.

Location: Mahalangur Himal sub-range of the Himalayas; summit straddles Nepal and Tibet.

Coordinates: 27°59′N, 86°55′E

Alternative Names: Sagarmatha (Nepali, "Forehead/Goddess of the Sky"), Chomolungma (Tibetan, "Goddess Mother of Mountains"), Peak XV.

Geological Formation: Formed ~60 million years ago by collision of Indian and Eurasian plates; grows ~4mm per year. Upper part composed of sedimentary/metamorphic rocks, with ancient marine fossils near summit.

Naming and Discovery

Origin of Name: Named in 1865 after Sir George Everest, surveyor general of India.

First Identified as Highest: Radhanath Sikdar (Great Trigonometrical Survey of India) in 1852.

Climbing and Routes

First Successful Ascent: May 29, 1953, by Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay.

Two Primary Routes: South Col Route (Nepal, standard), Northeast Ridge Route (Tibet/China).

The Death Zone: Above 8,000 meters (26,247 feet) — supplemental oxygen required.

Climbing Season: Pre-monsoon (April-May) and post-monsoon (late Sept-Nov).

Climate and Hazards

Temperatures below -30°C (-22°F), wind speeds over 200 km/h (125 mph). Main dangers: altitude sickness, avalanches, extreme cold, crevasses, exposure in Death Zone.

Fun Facts and Records

Not the tallest from base-to-peak (Mauna Kea is taller). Most summits: Sherpas like Kami Rita. Fastest ascent: under 11 hours from Base Camp. Expedition costs: $35,000–$100,000 USD. Many bodies remain on the mountain due to extreme conditions.
